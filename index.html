Architecture

Basic Model

Three competing productions select the sorting dimension. Selection follows utility-based conflict resolution with logistic noise. After feedback:

U_new = U_old + α(R - t - U_old)

Enhanced Model

Adds three mechanisms:

1. Hypothesis tracking: After errors, declarative memory recalls previously failed rules, suppressing their utility and boosting untried alternatives.

2. Attentional lapses: Random rule selection with probability p_lapse, modelling momentary disengagement.

3. Set maintenance decay: After sustained correct responding, utility can regress toward baseline, modelling WM maintenance failure.

Welcome to the WCST Cognitive Modelling Lab

This interactive tool lets you explore how a computational cognitive model learns to perform the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST) -- one of the most widely used neuropsychological tests of executive function.

You will see three progressively more sophisticated models, each adding new cognitive mechanisms. By comparing them against real human data from Heaton et al. (1993), you can see which aspects of cognition are needed to explain different patterns of behaviour.

Learning goal: Understand how reinforcement learning, hypothesis testing, attentional lapses, and asymmetric learning rates each contribute to human performance on the WCST.
What is the WCST?

The Wisconsin Card Sorting Test presents a participant with 128 cards that vary on three dimensions: colour, shape, and number. The participant must sort each card by one dimension (the "rule"), but is never told which rule is correct. The only feedback is "correct" or "incorrect" after each sort.

Here is the key challenge: after 10 consecutive correct sorts, the rule silently changes. The participant must detect this change and discover the new rule. The sequence follows: colour → shape → number → colour → shape → number (6 categories maximum).

Why does the WCST matter?

The WCST measures cognitive flexibility -- the ability to shift between mental strategies. It is sensitive to frontal lobe damage, making it a core tool in clinical neuropsychology. When patients with prefrontal lesions take the WCST, they show a characteristic pattern of perseverative errors: they keep sorting by the old rule even after it stops being rewarded.

What is ACT-R?

ACT-R (Adaptive Control of Thought -- Rational) is a cognitive architecture developed by John Anderson at Carnegie Mellon University. It models cognition as the interaction of several modules: procedural memory (production rules), declarative memory (facts), and a goal system. The key mechanism we use here is utility learning -- production rules compete for selection based on their expected reward, and these expectations update with experience.

The Three Models: A Progression of Cognitive Mechanisms

Each model below adds mechanisms to the previous one. Think of this as building up a theory of how the mind performs the WCST, one piece at a time.

1. Basic Model

Mechanism: Utility learning only

Three production rules compete to sort by colour, shape, or number. After each trial, the chosen rule's utility (expected value) is updated based on the reward received. Correct sorts increase utility; incorrect sorts decrease it. The rule with the highest utility (plus noise) fires next.

Analogy: Imagine a person who can only learn through trial and error, with no memory of what they already tried. They adjust their preferences slowly based on success and failure.

2. Enhanced Model

Adds: Hypothesis tracking + attentional lapses + set decay

Hypothesis tracking: After errors, the model recalls which rules failed before and avoids them. Attentional lapses: Occasionally picks a random rule (modelling distraction). Set decay: Even during a winning streak, the model sometimes "forgets" its current strategy.

Analogy: Now our person has a notepad to track failed hypotheses, but sometimes daydreams or loses their place.

3. Full Model

Adds: Asymmetric learning rates

Errors are weighted 1.8x more strongly than successes when updating utility. This captures the psychological finding that negative feedback is more salient than positive feedback -- a form of loss aversion applied to cognitive strategy selection.

Analogy: Our person now pays extra attention to mistakes, learning more quickly from "that was wrong" than from "that was right" (Bishara et al., 2010).

Goodness of Fit: RMSD Comparison

RMSD (Root Mean Square Deviation) measures how far each model's predictions are from the human data. Lower is better. Values are normalized by human standard deviation, so 1.0 means the model is off by exactly 1 SD.

What Do the RMSD Numbers Tell Us?

The RMSD is computed across 7 key WCST measures: categories completed, total errors, perseverative errors, non-perseverative errors, trials to complete the first category, conceptual level responses, and failure to maintain set. Each measure is normalised by the human standard deviation, so RMSD = 1.0 means the model is off by 1 SD on average.

BASIC Highest RMSD. Why? With only utility learning, the model gets the general pattern right (categories completed, rough error counts) but overshoots on perseverative errors and undershoots on non-perseverative errors and failure-to-maintain-set. The missing cognitive mechanisms show up as a large overall distance from human data.
ENHANCED Lower RMSD. Why? Adding hypothesis tracking reduces perseverative errors (closer to human), attentional lapses increase non-perseverative errors (closer to human), and set decay produces failure-to-maintain-set events (closer to human). Three separate mechanisms each improve a different measure.
FULL Lowest RMSD. Why? Asymmetric learning rates primarily improve perseverative errors (the biggest contributor to RMSD) by making the model abandon wrong rules faster. This single mechanism targets the measure where the Enhanced model still had the most room for improvement.
Key insight: The progression from Basic to Enhanced to Full illustrates a core principle of cognitive modelling: each mechanism in the architecture has a specific job. You cannot get the full picture with a single learning rule -- you need multiple interacting systems, just as the brain has multiple interacting regions.

Reading the Summary Table

The green-highlighted cells show which model is closest to the human value for each measure. Let's walk through the key measures:

Perseverative Errors (PE)

This is the most important WCST measure. A perseverative error occurs when the participant keeps sorting by a rule that is no longer correct. The human average is 11.5 errors.

The Basic model typically overshoots this (too many perseverative errors) because it can only "unlearn" the old rule through repeated negative feedback -- it has no way to remember what it already tried. The Enhanced model reduces PE by using hypothesis tracking to suppress previously-failed rules. The Full model reduces PE further because asymmetric learning rates mean the model reacts more strongly to errors, speeding up rule-switching.

Non-Perseverative Errors (NPE)

These are "random" errors -- sorting incorrectly but not by the previously-correct rule. The human average is 8.7. The Basic model tends to produce too few NPE because it has no source of random errors. The Enhanced model produces more NPE due to its attentional lapse mechanism, which occasionally causes random rule selection.

Failure to Maintain Set (FMS)

This measures how often a participant makes an error after getting 5+ consecutive correct sorts -- essentially "losing the plot" mid-streak. Humans average 0.66. The Enhanced model's set-decay mechanism produces this behaviour: even during a winning streak, there is a small probability the model forgets its current strategy.

Conceptual Level Responses (CLR%)

The percentage of responses that occur in runs of 3+ consecutive correct sorts. This measures sustained correct performance. Humans average 78.3%. A higher CLR% means the model can reliably maintain a strategy once found.

Key Metrics: Four-Way Comparison

How to read this chart

Each cluster of 4 bars is one WCST measure. The green bar is the human target -- you want model bars to match it.

Why the models differ -- metric by metric

Total Errors

BASIC Highest total errors. With only reward-based learning and no way to remember what was tried before, the model wastes many trials re-testing rules that already failed. Every rule switch costs ~5-8 extra trials of "unlearning."
ENHANCED Fewer errors overall, but gains some non-perseverative errors from attentional lapses. The net effect is lower total errors because hypothesis tracking eliminates many wasted trials after rule switches.
FULL Fewest errors. Asymmetric learning rates mean errors pack 1.8x the punch of successes. The model "hears" negative feedback louder, so it abandons wrong rules faster. This is the closest to human.

Perseverative Errors

BASIC Way too many. Why? After 10 correct sorts on one rule, its utility is very high. When the rule changes, the only way to switch is through repeated punishment -- which takes many trials. The model has no "insight" that the rule has changed.
ENHANCED Reduced. Why? Hypothesis tracking means that after an error, the model checks declarative memory: "Have I tried this rule before and failed?" If yes, it suppresses that rule and boosts untried alternatives. This is like a human thinking "colour stopped working -- let me try shape instead."
FULL Closest to human. Why? On top of hypothesis tracking, errors now cause a 1.8x stronger utility drop. The previously-correct rule's utility crashes faster, letting alternative rules overtake it sooner. Fewer trials are wasted perseverating.

Non-Perseverative Errors

BASIC Too few. Why? The basic model is purely deterministic (plus utility noise). It has no source of random errors -- it never "spaces out" or makes careless mistakes.
ENHANCED Closer to human. Why? The attentional lapse mechanism randomly selects a rule ~4% of the time, regardless of utility. These random selections produce non-perseverative errors -- mimicking how real humans occasionally lose focus during a long, repetitive task.

Failure to Maintain Set

BASIC Near zero. Why? Once the model locks onto the correct rule, utility noise almost never disrupts it. The model never "forgets" its current strategy.
ENHANCED Present. Why? Set maintenance decay occasionally pulls the winning rule's utility back toward baseline, even during a correct streak. This models the real-world phenomenon of working memory maintenance failure -- you know the rule, but sometimes you just lose track of it.

Conceptual Level Responses (%)

HUMAN Humans average ~78%. This measures sustained correct performance (runs of 3+ correct in a row). All three models tend to be within range of the human value, because once any model finds the correct rule, it maintains it well. Differences here reflect how quickly the model discovers the rule after each switch.
Think about it: Which measure shows the biggest difference between models? Which measure do all three models struggle with equally? What does this tell you about which cognitive mechanisms matter most for each aspect of WCST performance?

Error Breakdown: Perseverative vs Non-Perseverative

How to read this chart

Each stacked bar shows a model's total errors, split into perseverative (red, bottom) and non-perseverative (yellow, top). Look at both the height of each bar (total errors) and the ratio of red to yellow (error profile).

Why the stacked shapes differ
BASIC The bar is almost entirely red. Why? This model only makes errors when it perseverates on a previously-correct rule. It has no mechanism to produce random errors. Its error profile is too clean compared to humans -- real people have a messier mix of error types.
ENHANCED The yellow portion grows. Why? Two things happen: (1) hypothesis tracking shrinks the red portion by reducing perseveration, and (2) attentional lapses add yellow errors. The model now "spaces out" ~4% of the time, picking a random rule. These accidental errors are non-perseverative because they are not repeating the old rule -- they are random mistakes.
FULL Red shrinks further, ratio approaches human. Why? Asymmetric learning accelerates rule-switching, which reduces the perseverative portion even more. The bar height and ratio should now closely match the green human bar.
HUMAN ~57% perseverative, ~43% non-perseverative. The relatively high non-perseverative rate in humans reflects attentional lapses, exploratory hypothesis testing, and occasional confusion -- cognitive "noise" that a purely rational model would not produce.

Post-Switch Recovery Curve

How quickly does each model recover after the sorting rule changes?

How to read this chart

The x-axis shows trials after a rule switch (averaged across all 5 rule switches). The y-axis shows accuracy (0 = always wrong, 1 = always right). A steeper upward curve = faster recovery = less perseveration. The dashed green line is the human benchmark.

Why the recovery speeds differ

Trial 1: All models start near 0% accuracy. Why? The old rule just produced 10+ correct sorts in a row. Every model (and every human) tries it again first. This is not a failure -- it is rational given the information available.

BASIC Slowest recovery (flattest early curve). Why? The model must "unlearn" the old rule purely through repeated punishment. Each incorrect trial reduces the old rule's utility by a small amount. It takes ~5-7 trials of punishment before the old rule's utility drops below alternatives. The model is effectively "stuck" until the maths works out -- it has no strategic insight.
ENHANCED Faster recovery (steeper rise around trials 2-4). Why? After the first error, hypothesis tracking kicks in. The model recalls from declarative memory: "I just failed with colour. What have I not tried?" It then boosts the utility of untried rules, creating a shortcut that bypasses the slow unlearning process. This is analogous to a human switching hypotheses: "If colour is wrong, let me try shape."
FULL Fastest recovery (closest to the green human line). Why? In addition to hypothesis tracking, each error now delivers a 1.8x stronger blow to utility. The old rule's high utility gets demolished in just 2-3 errors instead of 5-7. Combined with the hypothesis boost for untried rules, the model can switch in as few as 2-3 trials -- almost as fast as humans.
HUMAN Fastest of all, especially at trials 2-3. Why? Humans have additional advantages the model does not fully capture: explicit hypothesis testing ("the rule must have changed"), working memory for recently-tried dimensions, and metacognitive awareness ("I know this test changes rules"). Some researchers believe humans can switch in as few as 1-2 trials using these deliberate strategies.
Think about it: If you were designing a cognitive model of someone with frontal lobe damage, what would you change? Frontal patients show very flat recovery curves -- they keep perseverating for many more trials. Hint: The prefrontal cortex supports both hypothesis testing (Enhanced model's DM mechanism) and learning from negative feedback (Full model's asymmetric LR). Disabling these mechanisms in the model would produce the flat curve seen in frontal patients.

Trial-by-Trial Accuracy smoothed, 5-trial window

How to read this chart

The x-axis is trial number (1-128). The y-axis is accuracy (averaged over all simulations). Look for the sawtooth pattern: accuracy rises as the model learns a rule, then drops sharply when the rule changes. Each "dip" = one rule switch. You should see 5 dips (6 categories, 5 transitions).

What to look for in each model's line
BASIC Deepest, widest dips. Why? Each rule switch creates a deep valley because the model must slowly unlearn the old rule through repeated punishment. The "valleys" are wide (spanning many trials) because recovery is slow. Notice the first dip is especially wide -- the model has never experienced a rule switch before and has no prior experience to draw on.
ENHANCED Shallower dips, quicker recovery. Why? Hypothesis tracking means the model can jump to an untried rule after just 1-2 errors, so the valleys are narrower. However, you may also notice the "peaks" between dips are slightly lower than the Basic model -- this is because attentional lapses occasionally pull accuracy down even when the model knows the correct rule.
FULL Shallowest dips, fastest recovery. Why? Asymmetric learning rates make each error cut deep into utility, so the old rule gets abandoned faster. The valleys are both shallower (less time at 0% accuracy) and narrower (fewer trials to recover).

Look for "learning-to-learn": In later rule switches (right side of the chart), the dips may be shallower than the early ones. Why? Rules that were correct before (e.g., colour in category 1) retain residual utility. When colour becomes correct again in category 4, the model already has a head start -- it does not need to learn from zero. This implicit benefit of prior experience is a form of transfer learning.

Think about it: If you smoothed the curve with a larger window (say 15 trials), the sawtooth would disappear and you would see a general upward trend. What does this mean psychologically? It suggests that over the full 128 trials, the model is getting better at the task overall -- not just at each individual rule, but at the process of switching rules. This is a hallmark of executive function.

Utility Traces (Single Representative Simulation) Basic

Select which model's utility trace to view. Switch between models to see how the same internal process looks different:

How to read this chart

Each coloured line tracks the utility value of one production rule (colour, shape, number) across all 128 trials. The rule with the highest utility at any given trial wins and gets selected. Think of utility as the model's "confidence" in each rule.

Visual guide to the WCST rule sequence:

Trials 1-10+: COLOUR → then SHAPE → then NUMBER → then COLOUR → then SHAPE → then NUMBER

What to look for when switching between models
BASIC Click "Basic" and look for:
  • Slow crossovers: When the correct rule changes, the old rule's line descends slowly while the new rule's line climbs. The gap between the descending and ascending lines stays large for many trials -- this is perseveration visualised.
  • Symmetric slopes: Lines go up at roughly the same rate they come down. Successes and failures have equal weight.
  • Large utility gaps: After 10 correct sorts, the winning rule is far above the others. This large gap is what makes switching so hard.
ENHANCED Click "Enhanced" and compare:
  • Sharper crossovers: When the rule changes, you should see the new rule's line jump upward more quickly. This is hypothesis tracking: after an error, untried rules get a utility boost, creating visible "kicks" upward.
  • Occasional dips in the winning line: Even during correct streaks, you may see brief downward spikes. These are set-decay events where the model momentarily "forgets" its strategy.
  • More jitter in all lines: The attentional lapse mechanism occasionally selects non-winning rules, which gives them small utility updates and creates noisier traces.
FULL Click "Full" and notice the key difference:
  • Asymmetric slopes -- this is the signature pattern. Lines fall steeply (errors hit 1.8x harder) but rise more gently (successes have normal weight). Visually, the "V-shapes" at rule switches are lopsided: fast descent, gradual ascent.
  • Faster crossovers: The old rule's utility crashes quickly, so alternative rules catch up in fewer trials. Crossovers happen in 2-3 trials instead of 5-7.
  • Narrower utility gaps: Because the winning rule gets punished harder when it fails, the gap between rules is smaller. Smaller gaps = easier switching = less perseveration.

What Have We Learned?

This modelling exercise demonstrates several important principles from cognitive psychology:

  • Perseveration is an emergent property of reward-based learning, not a separate mechanism that needs to be programmed in.
  • Utility learning alone is insufficient to explain the full pattern of human WCST performance -- we also need hypothesis tracking, attentional noise, and asymmetric error processing.
  • Each additional mechanism serves a specific function: hypothesis tracking reduces perseveration, attentional lapses produce non-perseverative errors, and asymmetric learning rates speed up recovery after rule switches.
  • The systematic mismatches between model and human data are informative -- they tell us what additional cognitive processes might be involved.
Clinical Implications

This model framework can be extended to clinical populations by adjusting specific parameters:

  • Frontal lobe damage: Reduce the learning rate (α) to model slower adaptation to feedback, or remove hypothesis tracking to simulate impaired strategic thinking.
  • ADHD: Increase attentional lapse rate and set-decay probability to model sustained attention difficulties.
  • Healthy ageing: Slightly reduce all parameters, reflecting general cognitive slowing.
